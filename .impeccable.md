# Design context — games.ofthepast.org

**The concept: the world's gaming table.** Where `games.ofancientrome.org` is the
*travertine* table — one city's boards scratched in Roman stone — this is the
*same impulse to play, everywhere and always*: a worn boxwood-and-parchment
surface on which the games of every people are laid out side by side. Senet from
Egypt, Go from China, a mancala from Ghana, a star-board from Aotearoa, a dreidel
from the Ashkenazi diaspora — all resting on one table. It is sibling to the Rome
games site (same warmth, same incised boards, same "shelf of boards, not icons")
and to `rulers.ofthepast.org` (same move from one culture to the whole world),
and it wears the `ofthepast` family face.

## Palette

- **Ground:** parchment / boxwood — warm bone (`--stone` `#efe6d2` light; deep
  `#16110b` dark). Raised panels and sunk "niches" are one and two steps darker.
  The board thumbnails sit in a recessed niche, as if inlaid into the table.
- **Accents:** **terracotta** `#a8412e` (the primary — the colour of a counter
  and of the play button) and **bronze/gold** `#b88a3e` (hairlines, the
  reconstruction tag, focus rings). The gold hairline is the thread shared with
  both siblings.
- **Game-family pigments** carry meaning and are never mere fills — a dot, a
  rotated square, a chip edge, the active filter:
  - war & strategy → **olive** `#7a8a3a`
  - hunt & siege → **russet** `#9c5a2e`
  - race & tables → **terracotta** `#b4472e`
  - sowing → **earth-green** `#3f7a55`
  - alignment → **tessera blue** `#3a6ea5`
  - dice & lots → **gold** `#caa05a`
  - hand & guess → **amethyst** `#8a5a8a`
- **Counters** are bone (`album`) and terracotta/slate for opposed sides; seeds
  are earth-brown; stones black and ivory. These inks read across boards, the
  pieces legend and the pills.

## Type

- **Display:** Cormorant Garamond — the masthead, game names, section and lane
  headers. An elegant high-contrast serif that belongs to neither one place nor
  one century — the right register for a *world* catalogue (and the face of the
  `ofthepast` sibling). Used at 600–700 weight.
- **Body:** Spectral — a humanist serif with enough warmth for long rules and
  history without feeling like a manuscript (shared with both siblings).
- Fallbacks: Marcellus SC / Cardo / Georgia.

## The boards

Every board is a generated inline **SVG** — incised lines on a parchment field.
This is deliberate: it ties the visual language to the *real* boards (which often
survive precisely as lines scratched on stone, wood or cloth), and it keeps the
repo asset-free. Each game-family draws true to its own form: the line-grids of
Go and the chess cousins; the alquerque lattice shared by alquerque and bagh-chal;
the besieged centre of the tafl board; the rosette-studded twenty squares of Ur;
the boustrophedon of senet's thirty; the cross-and-circle of pachisi and the
Aztec cross of patolli; the inward spiral of the Goose; the pitted rows of the
mancalas; the concentric squares of nine men's morris and the eight-pointed star
of mū tōrere; and, for the dice and hand games, a bare table with a ghosted word
(VENVS, HAZARD, MORRA) the way a real gaming surface was captioned.

## Motion & restraint

- Cards lift a few pixels on hover and take on their family pigment at the border.
  The detail overlay rises and fades once. Nothing loops, nothing pulses — the
  stillness of a table waiting for players, broken only when *you* pick up a piece
  (which happens in the launcher, not here).
- The site itself never animates a board. It is a shelf; the *table* is the
  launcher.

## Voice

Plain, a little wry, scholarly but not stuffy — the register of someone who has
read Murray and Parlett and wants you to *play*. Even-handed across cultures: no
game is exotic, none is the default; each is introduced on its own terms with its
own people named. Where rules are lost the voice is explicit and honest about it
(the reconstruction banner is not hidden in small print; recovered cases like Ur
and tablut are explained). The footer is the OHM house line, always: *Made with
<3 in Bologna by OpenHistoryMap.*

## What "good" looks like here

A visitor lands, immediately sees the boards as boards (not icons), reads one
tagline, sees *which people, which age*, clicks, understands in ten seconds *what
the game is, when and where it was played, and how* — and then opens a real table
with a friend in one more click. The history grounds the play; the play earns the
history; and the whole world is on the table at once.
